Coldwater Creek & West Lake Landfill

Raising awareness about radioactive contamination in St. Louis, supporting affected families, and demanding accountability and cleanup.

What

The Issue

Radioactive waste from the Manhattan Project was improperly stored and disposed of in the St. Louis area. Over decades, contamination spread into soil and waterways, including Coldwater Creek, affecting nearby communities.

Where

Communities Affected

Bridgeton, Florissant, Hazelwood, and surrounding North St. Louis County neighborhoods—many built on or near contaminated land.

When

How We Got Here

1942: During the rush to build the first atomic bomb, the United States hired Mallinckrodt Chemical Works to process uranium at its downtown St. Louis plant.

1946: After World War II, the federal government acquired land near the St. Louis airport to store radioactive byproduct residue from uranium processing at Mallinckrodt’s facilities. The waste was stored outdoors in deteriorating drums, exposed to wind and rain. By 1949, the material threatened to contaminate nearby Coldwater Creek, but officials believed moving the drums posed too great a risk to workers.

1966: The government found a buyer for the thousands of tons of nuclear waste stored near the airport. Most of the material was moved to a site on Latty Avenue in Hazelwood, where it was again left exposed to the elements. Cotter Corporation dried the waste and shipped usable material to its facility in Colorado; however, the Latty Avenue site remained contaminated for decades.

1973: Several tons of radioactive material were illegally dumped into West Lake Landfill. After Cotter Corporation finished processing the waste, it was left with approximately 8,700 tons of leached barium sulfate containing several tons of uranium. With federal approval, Cotter disposed of this waste by mixing it with 39,000 tons of contaminated topsoil from the Latty Avenue site and dumping it at the West Lake Landfill in Bridgeton.

2010: Adjacent to the radiologically contaminated West Lake Landfill is the Bridgeton Landfill, where an underground fire began smoldering. The fire intensified by 2013, producing strong odors and drawing public attention to West Lake due to fears that the fire could spread to the nearby buried radioactive waste.

Why It Matters

Ongoing Risk

The radioactive waste currently present requires proper long-term containment. Studies show that this radioactive waste will increase 9 times over the next 200 years, extending risk to future generations if not properly gotten rid of.

Health Effects

Long term exposure to radiation has been linked to very rare and aggressive cancers and other illnesses reported by residents who lived near Coldwater Creek and the West Lake landfill.

Environmental Damage

Contamination washed into waterways and soil, spreading beyond original storage sites and into residential areas.

Community Trauma

Families have faced illness, loss, and uncertainty, often without clear warnings from the government.

In Memory of Eleana Waide

“Loved and remembered.”

Eleana Waide lived near the West Lake Landfill and found out she had developed osteosarcoma, which is a type of bone cancer, on January 7th, 2010. In November of 2010 she was declared cancer free and got to ring the bell. But in the summer of 2011 the doctors found out that the cancer had spread to her lungs. My uncle loved her so much, he drove her all over the country trying to find a cure with new experimental drugs, but unfortunately my Aunt Ellie passed away in her home on April 3, 2014. This website was created to help those suffering just like I am, and to bring awareness to those who have lost loved ones. I miss you every day Aunt Ellie. I wish you were still here.
